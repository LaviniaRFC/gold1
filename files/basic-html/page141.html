I was a very lucky child, because although I was born in the early 80s, I somehow get the feeling that I grew up in the 60s. Shortly after I was born, my parents moved away from Los Angeles and settled in a small town named Spring Valley in the Midwest. It was one of those towns that seemed to have stopped in time. We lived in a simple, but big house, on a street full of houses that looked just like it. We were made to feel so welcome there that my dad never even considered moving, even after their financial situation became more comfortable.
We had a routine that almost resembled a sitcom. I woke up, we had breakfast together, my dad would leave for work, and my brother and I got on the bus to go to school. Mom worked as a writer, so she was lucky enough to work from home. After school, we would ride our bikes around the neighborhood or play ball until it began to get dark. As soon as my Dad got home, we’d have dinner and then spend some quality time doing stuff together. It seems very corny nowadays, but I treasure these memories. The years we spent in that little town helped me shape the values of the person I would become.
Since it was a very small town, we sort of knew everybody, and everyone took care of each other. I remember Ms. Peterson, from the grocery store, would always send me home if she caught me out riding my bike after 6:00 p.m. My mother became an influential member in the community and was appointed head of the school council, and some years later she was chosen to lead the town council. As for my dad, he worked for some time at an accounting office and then started his own accounting firm.
I moved out of Spring Valley when I went to college, but I think it’s fair to say that the city never truly left me. I’m in a serious relationship now, and I imagine in some years’ time I’ll be married with children. It won’t surprise me if, when that happens, I decide to move back to my little town. And since my mother will probably be re-elected mayor, chances are I will be very welcome.
